Crash Course into Reefing

This crash course is organized into eight phases to introduce the basics of planning, building, and caring for your new reef aquarium as it matures. Remember we are building our own ecosystem from the ground up and having the knowledge to not only build it but also caring for it is paramount. Read each section to better understand every piece of this hobby so that you can start off on the right foot and make life much easier for you and your aquarium. Follow the order and take your time. Stability beats speed.

Before You Start: What is an Aquarium System?

A reef aquarium is a miniature ocean, a living ecosystem that we personally create within our homes. The water acts as the ocean itself, carrying oxygen and nutrients to everything that lives within it. FIlters and pums keep the water moving and clean, while the heater keeps it warm like the tropics and the lights provide the energy corals need to grow just as sunlight fuels reefs in nature Every part of the system that you choose will work together to keep life stable. In this guide, every section will explain why these pieces matter and how to choose them for your reef.

Plan & Gear

Before water, fish, or coral ever touch our aquarium, you’re designing an environment that has to stay balanced every day. Your plan decides how easy the system will be to care for, how safe it is for your animals, and how forgiving it will be when something goes wrong. A reef tank is not just decoration but a small ocean that depends on stable gear, thoughtful layout, and a realistic budget. Getting these things right now prevents expensive or stressful changes later.

1. Understanding the Foundation

Think of your aquarium as a living machine. Every piece of equipment plays a role in keeping the environment balanced: the tank holds your ecosystem; filtration removes waste; flow delivers oxygen and nutrients; heating maintains temperatures; and lighting fuels coral growth just as sunlight feeds reefs in nature. When these systems work together, you create stability and stability is the key to success.

2. Choosing Your Tank Type

Start small enough to manage, but large enough to be forgiving. For most beginners, a 20–40 gallon All-In-One (AIO) tank is ideal. It’s compact, tidy, and includes built-in chambers for your heater, pump, and filter media. No plumbing required.

If you have more room and want flexibility, consider a sump system. A sump is a second tank hidden inside the stand that holds extra water volume and equipment. It keeps the display clean, increases stability, and allows for add-ons like a skimmer or refugium. The trade-off is higher cost and a bit more setup work.

3. Planning Your Budget

It’s tempting to start buying immediately, but a smart budget avoids wasted money and mismatched parts. Divide your plan into categories:

Category Purpose Beginner Tip
Tank & Stand Holds and supports the system Ensure the stand is level and rated for weight

Heater Keeps stable temperature (78–80°F) 3–5 watts per gallon

Buy the best-quality gear you can reasonably afford. Reliability matters more than brand, and a quiet, efficient system will save you endless headaches.

4. Designing Your Layout

Before adding water, visualize the system like a blueprint: leave space for cords, hoses, and maintenance access. Create drip loops so water can’t travel down cables into outlets. Secure your rockwork so nothing topples once fish or snails start exploring. Plan for noise control — pumps and overflows should hum quietly, not roar.

When placing live rock, think structure first, looks second. Build interlocking, stable arches or islands that allow water flow to move through them. Avoid tall, narrow towers that can collapse. A well-planned layout means safer animals, easier cleaning, and long-term enjoyment.

Recap

  • Decide early between AIO (simple) or Sump (expandable).
  • Plan your budget in categories, not all at once.
  • Build for stability, access, and quiet operation.
  • Prioritize safe structure and organized cables.
  • Learn the key terms — understanding your gear is half the hobby.
  • Remember: the goal is a system that runs smoothly so you can enjoy the life inside it.

Water & Salt

Consistency is key! Every reef lives and dies by water quality. Tap water and inconsistent salt mixes create unpredictable chemistry that leads to algae and coral stress. Start with pure RODI water and keep a consistent salt recipe so your animals can trust the water, day after day.

1. Start with Pure Water

For reef aquariums, tap water is a trap. It can contain chlorine/chloramine, nitrates, phosphates, and metals that feed algae and harm invertebrates. Use RODI water (Reverse Osmosis + Deionization) to bring TDS (Total Dissolved Solids) near zero before any salt is added.

Term Meaning Why It Matters
RODI Filtration that removes nearly all dissolved solids from tap water. Prevents nuisance algae and chemistry swings from contaminants.
TDS Total Dissolved Solids (ppm) remaining after filtration. Keep ≤ 5 ppm; ideally 0 ppm for reef tanks.
DI Resin Final RODI stage that “polishes” water by removing leftover ions. Replace when your TDS starts to rise to maintain purity.

Getting RODI water: buy pre-filtered RODI from a local fish store, or install a small 4-stage home RODI unit (faucet or hose hookup). It’s one of the best long-term investments in the hobby.

2. Mix Your Saltwater

The ocean averages 35 ppt salinity (1.025–1.026 SG). Mix saltwater in a separate bucket/barrel, never in the display! Use a small pump for circulation and a heater to ~78 °F (25.5 °C) while mixing.

  1. Fill a clean container with RODI water.
  2. Add a small circulation pump and heater (to ~78 °F).
  3. Slowly add your salt mix while water circulates.
  4. Test salinity with a refractometer (more accurate than swing hydrometers).
  5. Follow mixing instructions for your choice of salt to fully dissolve and stabilize pH.
  6. Adjust salinity with RODI (to lower) or more salt (to raise).

3. Match Salt Mix to Your Plan

Different salts have different baseline alkalinity. Pick one that fits your long-term livestock goals and stick with it. Remember staying consistent is extremely important. Switching salts often will throw your tank off-balance and will only cause your fish and corals to stress.

Salt Type Typical Alkalinity (dKH) Ideal For Notes
Standard Reef Mix (e.g., IO, Red Sea Blue) 7–9 Softies & LPS Balanced, stable, easy to maintain.
Pro / Growth Mix (e.g., TM Pro, Red Sea Coral Pro) 9–11 SPS & Mixed Higher alkalinity supports faster calcification.
Low-Alk Ultra Pure (e.g., AF Reef Salt, Fritz RPM Blue) 6.5–8 Dosed SPS Designed for automated dosing or Ca reactors.

Tip: set your alkalinity target before the cycle. Switching salt later can cause avoidable swings.

4. Foundation Parameters

Keep these within stable ranges. Stability beats perfection so try not to fall in the trap of chasing exact numbers.

Parameter Target Range Notes
Salinity 35 ppt (1.025–1.026 SG) Calibrate your refractometer monthly with 35 ppt solution.
Temperature 77–80 °F (25–27 °C) Avoid daily swings > 2 °F.
Alkalinity (dKH) 8–12 Buffers pH and supports skeletal growth.
Calcium (Ca) 400–450 ppm Consumed by corals; test weekly in stocked tanks.
Magnesium (Mg) 1250–1350 ppm Prevents Ca/Alk precipitation; stabilizes chemistry.
pH 7.9–8.3 Tracks overall gas balance; improves with fresh air & skimming.

5. Maintenance Tips

  • Use RODI only for top-off (evaporation). Never add saltwater for top-off.
  • Shoot for consisten 15-20% water changes every 2 weeks to replenish nutrients absorbed by your corals
  • Store mixed saltwater with a lid and small circulation pump if not used immediately.
  • Keep spare DI resin, RODI filters, and 35 ppt calibration fluid on hand.
  • Change salt brands only if you have a reason — consistency helps corals thrive.

Recap

  • Start with RODI water (0 TDS) for mixing, top-offs, and water changes.
  • Mix salt to 35 ppt (1.025–1.026 SG) and keep it consistent.
  • Choose a salt type that matches your reef plan (softies/LPS vs SPS).
  • Hold core parameters steady; stability > perfection.
  • Calibrate tools and test regularly to prevent silent drifts.

Setup Day

Setup Day turns planning into action. This is where you test stability, confirm a leak-free system, and place equipment so the tank runs safely for years. Take it slow; neat layout now prevents headaches later.

1. Stand & Tank Placement

  • Level the stand left-right and front-back. Shim under the stand (never under the tank).
  • Set the tank centered on the stand, leaving 2–3" behind it for hoses and cables.
  • Inspect glass (seams/chips) before water goes in.

2. Aquascape (Dry)

Build your rockwork dry with superglue gel, epoxy, or rods. Push from multiple angles—if it doesn’t move dry, it’s safe when wet. Keep ½–1" off the glass for flow and cleaning. Photograph the layout as your blueprint.

Figure 1 — Stable arches/islands, gap from glass, heater near flow, return aimed across the surface.

Pro Tip — make a cardboard cutout of the inside floor of your tank. This gives you a great reference while building your aquascape outside of your aquarium.

3. Substrate

Rinse dry sand in buckets until water is mostly clear (skip rinsing for “live sand”). Spread 1–2" even depth; deeper beds can trap waste.

4. Fill & Test (Both Builds)

  1. Fill with your pre-mixed saltwater slowly to minimize sand clouds.
  2. Start heater and return pump once submerged; verify flow direction and surface movement.
  3. pay attention to water level after starting return pump. Pause as needed to add/remove water.
  4. Run at least 24 hours. Check seams, bulkheads, hose barbs, unions, and under the stand for leaks.
  5. Lights off. You’re growing bacteria, not algae.

5. Cable Safety & ATO

  • Create drip loops on every cord; mount power strips up off the floor.
  • *If purchased* Follow your ATO manufacture's placement instructions. Test fill/stop behavior.
  • Label critical cords (heater, return, lights) for quick troubleshooting later.

Figure 4 — Drip loops keep water out of outlets; ATO reservoir feeds the return chamber only.

Recap

  • Level stand; inspect glass; lock the aquascape dry; rinse sand.
  • Sump: tune drains, place skimmer/heater in fixed-depth section, ATO sensor in return.
  • AIO: basket (floss→carbon→GFO), heater mid-chamber, return pump + ATO sensor in final chamber.
  • Fill with 35 ppt, start heat/return, leak-check 24h with lights off.
  • Cable management with drip loops; label and route neatly.

Cycle & First Tests

The nitrogen cycle is the foundation of reef life. Before fish or coral can survive, your system needs beneficial bacteria that convert toxic waste into safer forms. Rushing this process — or skipping it — leads to deadly ammonia spikes that can wipe out everything you’ve built. Cycling is how you teach your tank to breathe. This is where patience pays off: biology, not equipment, decides when your tank is ready.

1. What “Seeding” Means

“Seeding” a tank means introducing live bacteria to start the biological engine that runs your reef. These bacteria live on every surface like your rock, sand, and filter media and they process fish waste, leftover food, and other organic matter. There are two main ways to seed:

MethodDescriptionProsNotes
Bottled BacteriaCommercial starter cultures (e.g., Dr. Tim’s, Fritz, Brightwell).Fast, clean, predictable.Follow dosage; keep refrigerated.
Live Rock / SandMaterial from established systems carrying microbes.Natural diversity.May import pests or algae.

These bacteria colonize porous surfaces (like rock) and begin transforming ammonia into safer compounds. That transformation is the cycle.

2. The Nitrogen Cycle

All organic waste eventually becomes ammonia (NH₃), which is toxic to fish and invertebrates. Specialized bacteria convert ammonia into nitrite (NO₂⁻), which is also toxic. A second group converts nitrite into nitrate (NO₃⁻), which is far less harmful and can be removed with water changes or absorbed by algae.

StageCompoundHandled ByToxicityDescription
1️⃣ AmmoniaNH₃ / NH₄⁺NitrosomonasHighProduced from waste and decay or bottled ammonia.
2️⃣ NitriteNO₂⁻Nitrobacter / NitrospiraMediumIntermediate step; spikes mid-cycle.
3️⃣ NitrateNO₃⁻Algae / Water ChangesLowFinal product; removed by water changes.

This process takes time — usually 2 to 5 weeks, depending on temperature, bacteria density, and ammonia source.

3. Starting the Cycle

  1. Turn everything on. Heater, return and circulation pumps. keep lights off, helps with algae control.
  2. Add bacteria according to the product's dosage.
  3. Add an ammonia source (bottled ammonia or a pinch of food) so bacteria have "food".
  4. begin testing after 48 hours and every 2–3 days.

4. Testing & Tracking

Test the following every 2–3 days using reliable kits (Salifert, Red Sea, etc.):

ParameterSymbolSafe LevelPurpose
AmmoniaNH₃ / NH₄⁺0 ppmToo high = still cycling.
NitriteNO₂⁻0 ppmAppears mid-cycle; falls to 0 near completion.
NitrateNO₃⁻<30 ppmShows cycle is producing end-stage waste.
Temperature°F / °C77–80 °FStable warmth speeds bacteria growth.
Salinityppt / SG35 ppt / 1.025Keep steady for microbial health.

5. Example Cycle Timeline

A simple cycle timeline often looks like this:

DaysAmmoniaNitriteNitrateWhat to Do
0–2Rising00Add bacteria + ammonia.
3–7HighStarting0Keep testing; do not add livestock.
8–14DroppingRising0–10Nitrite spike – normal.

When both ammonia and nitrite hit zero and nitrate is measurable, your biological cycle is complete.

6. Visual Clues

  • Cloudy water: harmless bacterial bloom; clears naturally.
  • Brown dust (diatoms): first algae stage; feeds on silicates.
  • Green film: early algae—normal, means nitrate exists.
  • Microbubbles: gas from bacterial metabolism.

Resist overreacting — this phase is nature balancing itself. Do not scrub, vacuum, or dose chemicals.

7. Finishing the Cycle

When ammonia and nitrite remain at 0 for 5–7 days and nitrate is measurable: perform a 20–30% water change, keep lightly feeding bacteria until livestock arrive, and prepare a cleanup crew (snails/hermits). Complete a baseline pH test before moving forward if your aquarium will be a fish only with live rock (FOWLR) set up. Include baseline Alkalinity and Calcium tests if you will be adding corals.

8. Key Terms

Recap

  • Add bacteria + ammonia source to seed your biofilter.
  • Test NH₃ / NO₂ / NO₃ every 2–3 days.
  • “Ready” = ammonia = 0, nitrite = 0, nitrate > 0.
  • Do a partial water change, then add your cleanup crew.
  • Expect early algae blooms—don’t panic, they’re normal.

Flow, Filtration & Nutrients

Flow and filtration work together to keep your tank alive. Flow brings oxygen to every coral, sweeps waste toward your filters, and prevents “dead zones” where detritus settles and decays. Filtration then removes that waste before it can rot and release nutrients. Stable, predictable nutrient levels keep corals colorful and algae under control. If water movement is the reef’s pulse, filtration is its liver.

1. Understanding Flow

Flow is more than water movement, it’s how the reef breathes. Corals exchange oxygen and nutrients through the thin boundary layer of water that touches their tissue. Without proper flow, that layer becomes stagnant and suffocates coral cells.

Coral TypeFlow GoalExample Setup
Soft / LPS10–20× turnoverTwo opposing wave pumps alternating sides.
SPS30–60× turnoverFour pumps or a gyre system creating random turbulence.
Fish-Only5–10× turnoverOne return pump + small circulation pump.

Example: A 40-gallon LPS reef should circulate roughly 400–800 gallons per hour total inside the tank. That means two 400 GPH pumps are better than one 800 GPH pump. For this setup, multiple sources create randomness.

2. Flow Patterns

PatternDescriptionBest ForNotes
LaminarSingle constant stream.Frag tanks.Can damage coral tissue if too direct.
Randomized / GyreAlternating, sweeping motion.All corals.Mimics natural reef surge; prevents dead spots.
Pulse / WaveOn/off rhythm.LPS, softies.Creates gentle sway; watch for surface splash.

Tip: point your wave pumps slightly upward toward the surface. This breaks the surface tension in the water as well as any surface films to improve gas exchange. Not only will this provide more oxygen for you tank but it was also help raise your PH levels if they're low.

3. Filtration Layers

Filtration is how the tank removes or neutralizes waste before it poisons the system. Think of it as three layers working together.

TypePurposeExamples
MechanicalTraps debris and detritus.Filter floss, socks, rollers.
BiologicalHosts bacteria that process waste.Rock, bio-media, sand.
ChemicalRemoves dissolved pollutants.Carbon, GFO, resins.

Ideal Filtration order: Intake → Mechanical → Skimmer → (Optional Refugium) → Return Pump

4. Protein Skimmer

A protein skimmer uses bubbles to pull dissolved waste and organics out of the water before they break down. It also boosts oxygen exchange which iscrucial as bioload grows.

When you should add a skimmer:

  1. After your tank is cycled and producing measurable nitrate.
  2. If you feed heavily or notice surface film or rising nutrients.
  3. In nano tanks, water changes alone may suffice initially.

5. Nutrient Balance

Corals need nitrate (NO₃) and phosphate (PO₄) in small amounts. Zero nutrients starve corals, while excess fuels algae. The goal is balance, not total removal. Other important nutrients are Calcium (Ca), Alkalinity (dKH) and Magnesium (Mg)

ParameterTarget RangeSourceControl
Nitrate (NO₃)2–20 ppmFish waste, foodWater changes, refugium, dosing.
Phosphate (PO₄)0.01–0.2 ppmFood, leaching rockGFO, water changes, macroalgae.
Calcium (Ca)350–4500 ppmSalt, calcium reactor, dosingCoral consumption, water changes
Alkalinity (dKH)7–12 ppmSalt, KalkwasserCoral consumption, water changes.
Magnesium (Mg)1250-1350 ppmSalt, dosingCoral consumption, water changes.

Try to shoot for a ratio of 100:1. If your NO₃ is at 5ppm then your PO₄ should be .05ppm. Ca, dKH & Mg go hand in hand. If you are on the higher range of Ca then you also want dKH and Mg on the higher side of the range.

Consistency > Perfection Don’t chase numbers daily. Track weekly trends and correct gradually. A stable nutrient baseline produces predictable coral color and growth.

6. Optional Upgrades

Add-OnPurposeBenefit
RefugiumA small “safe zone” in your sump where macroalgae (like chaeto) and microfauna like copepods grow.Removes nitrate & phosphate naturally.
GFO ReactorUses Granular Ferric Oxide to bind phosphates.Reduces algae growth; clarifies water.
Carbon ReactorRemoves discoloration and toxins released by corals.Removes toxins & discoloration.

Tip: Add these only after your tank has matured and your nutrients are stable. Early over-filtration can and will starve your corals of needed nutrients.

7. Common Terms

Recap

  • Target 10–20× flow for soft/LPS, 30–60× for SPS.
  • Use randomized, non-laminar flow to avoid dead spots.
  • Filter order: mechanical → skimmer → optional refugium/reactors → return.
  • Keep nutrients steady: NO₃ = 5–20 ppm, PO₄ = 0.03–0.1 ppm.
  • Clean filter media often; consistency keeps the reef healthy.

Stocking & Acclimation

This is where your reef becomes alive, but also where patience matters most. Adding livestock too quickly, skipping quarantine, or neglecting acclimation can undo weeks of careful cycling and preparation. Each fish, coral, and invertebrate brings unseen microorganisms. some can be helpful, but some are harmful. Your goal is to introduce life slowly, safely, and intentionally, giving your new inhabitants the best chance to thrive.

1. Stocking Order

Ensure that your tank is read by checking that Ammonia & Nitrates are 0 and Nitrates are measureable but not too high (<30). Add animals in stages to give bacteria time to adjust and prevent ammonia spikes.

PhaseWhat to AddPurpose
1️⃣ Clean-Up CrewSnails, hermits, conchsEat algae and waste; stabilize microfauna.
2️⃣ Hardy FishClownfish, goby, blennyHandle minor parameter swings; low aggression.
3️⃣ Sensitive FishWrasses, tangs, dwarf angelsRequire stable water and mature biofilter.
4️⃣ CoralsSofties → LPS → SPSGradually increase biological demand.
5️⃣ SpecialtyAnemones, clams, mandarinsOnly after 6+ months of stability.

Tip: Add one or two fish at a time, and wait at least 2–3 weeks between additions. This gives your biofilter time to adjust to the new bioload.

2. Quarantine (QT)

Quarantine is optional but highly recommended. It gives you control and the ability observe, detect, and treat disease before fish enter the display.

MethodProsConsSetup Tips
With QT (Recommended) Prevents parasites, allows treatment, reduces display stress. Requires extra tank and monitoring. 10–20 gal bare-bottom, sponge filter, heater, PVC hiding tubes.
Without QT Cheaper, simpler. High disease risk; difficult to treat once fish are in display. Buy only from trusted, disease-free suppliers.

Recommended QT Time: 14–21 days for observation and optional preventive treatment. If symptoms return, restart the QT time and continue for another 14-21 days

3. Drip Acclimation (Fish & Inverts)

  1. Float the sealed bag for 15–20 min to equalize temperature.
  2. Pour bag contents into a clean bucket.
  3. every 5 minutes pour a portion of your tank water until you've doubled the water volume. Roughly 20-30 minutes.
  4. Gently net or hand-transfer livestock into the display. Never pour store water.

Tip: Turn off lights during introduction to reduce stress.

4. Coral Dipping

Corals carry microscopic pests. Always dip before adding to your tank.

Coral TypeExampleSensitivityDip Time
SoftZoanthids, mushroomsLow5–7 min
LPSHammer, torch, frogspawnMedium5–10 min
SPSAcropora, montiporaHigh≈5 min

Use products like Revive, Coral RX, or Bayer per instructions. Dip in tank water, rinse in clean water, and discard dip solution after use.

5. Observation After Adding

  • Dim lights for a few hours to reduce stress.
  • Watch breathing and swimming; heavy gilling = stress or ammonia.
  • Feed lightly at first; let new fish settle.
  • Move new corals slowly upward over 1–2 weeks for light acclimation.

6. Key Terms

TermDefinition
Quarantine (QT)Temporary isolation for observation/treatment of new fish.
Drip AcclimationSlowly mixing tank and shipping water to avoid shock.
Coral DipAntipest rinse for corals before adding to display.
CUCClean-Up Crew—snails/crabs that manage waste and algae.
BioloadTotal waste produced by tank inhabitants.

Recap

  • Add livestock gradually: CUC → hardy fish → sensitive fish → corals → specialty species.
  • Quarantine fish 14–21 days whenever possible.
  • Drip acclimate new fish/inverts; dip corals before placement.
  • Dim lights and monitor behavior during introduction.
  • Patience = long-term success.

Maintenance & Upgrades

You have successfully created a living ecosystem in your reef tank and its success depends less on expensive gear and more on consistent habits. Routine testing, cleaning, and observation prevent 90% of reef problems before they start. When you maintain rhythm, your reef rewards you with stability and a beautiful display.

1. Daily & Weekly Rhythm

TaskFrequencyPurpose
Feed livestock mindfullyDailyAvoid overfeeding and excess nutrients.

Reef Glossary

System & Equipment

  • AIO (All-In-One) — Display tank with built-in rear filtration chambers; no external sump.
  • Sump — Separate tank in the stand that adds water volume and hides gear.
  • Return Pump — Pushes water from sump to display; helps set turnover (3–5×/hr).
  • Powerhead / Wavemaker — In-tank circulation pump for internal flow.
  • Protein Skimmer — Removes dissolved organics with foam fractionation; boosts oxygenation.
  • Refugium — Low-flow zone (often in sump) growing macroalgae/pods for natural nutrient export.
  • ATO (Auto Top-Off) — Adds fresh water to offset evaporation and keep salinity stable.
  • Media Reactor — Chamber that forces water through media (e.g., carbon, GFO).
  • GFO — Granular Ferric Oxide; binds phosphate (PO₄).

Planning, Layout & Safety

  • Aquascape — Rock structure for stability, flow, and coral placement.
  • Drip Loop — Cable routing below the outlet to prevent water intrusion.
  • Turnover Rate — Return flow ÷ display gallons (target ~3–5×/hr).
  • Total Flow — (Return + powerheads) ÷ gallons; varies by coral type.

Water & Salt

  • RODI — Reverse Osmosis + Deionization; near-zero TDS source water.
  • TDS — Total Dissolved Solids (ppm); aim ≤5, ideally 0 for mixing.
  • DI Resin — Final RODI stage that “polishes” water; replace when TDS rises.
  • Salinity — 35 ppt (~1.025–1.026 SG); measure with refractometer.
  • Refractometer — Optical tool for salinity; calibrate with 35 ppt fluid.

Core Parameters

  • Alkalinity (dKH) — Buffers pH; supports skeletal growth (target ~8–12 dKH).
  • Calcium (Ca) — Coral skeleton component (target ~400–450 ppm).
  • Magnesium (Mg) — Helps prevent Ca/Alk precipitation (target ~1250–1350 ppm).
  • pH — Acidity/alkalinity (typ. 7.9–8.3).
  • Temperature — 77–80 °F (25–27 °C) with minimal daily swing.

Nitrogen Cycle

  • Seed — Introduce live bacteria (bottled or live rock/sand) to start cycling.
  • Ammonia (NH₃/NH₄⁺) — Toxic waste; first stage of the cycle.
  • Nitrite (NO₂⁻) — Toxic intermediate; spikes mid-cycle.
  • Nitrate (NO₃⁻) — End product; controlled by water changes/algae uptake.
  • Cycle Complete — Ammonia = 0, Nitrite = 0, Nitrate present.
  • Biofilter — Surfaces (rock/sand/media) where bacteria colonize.
  • Diatoms — Early brown film algae; common during/after cycling.

Flow, Filtration & Nutrients

  • Laminar Flow — Straight, constant stream; can be harsh if direct.
  • Random/Gyre Flow — Alternating, sweeping currents; reduces dead spots.
  • Pulse/Wave — On/off rhythm; gentle sway (popular for LPS/softies).
  • Dead Spot — Area with little/no flow where detritus accumulates.
  • Bioload — Total waste production from livestock.
  • Nitrate (NO₃) — Target ~2–20 ppm for reefs.
  • Phosphate (PO₄) — Target ~0.01–0.2 ppm (keep balanced with NO₃).

Lighting

  • PAR — Photosynthetically Active Radiation; usable light for corals.
  • Spectrum — Blend of wavelengths (blue/UV heavy for photosynthesis).
  • Photoperiod — Daily duration of light.
  • Kelvin (K) — Color temperature (10–14K daylight to bluer reef looks).
  • Bleaching — Loss of symbiotic algae due to excess light/heat/stress.
  • FOWLR — Fish Only With Live Rock (light mainly for viewing/aesthetics).

Stocking & Acclimation

  • CUC (Clean-Up Crew) — Snails/hermits that manage algae and waste.
  • Quarantine (QT) — Temporary isolation to observe/treat fish.
  • Drip Acclimation — Gradual mixing of tank and shipping water.
  • Coral Dip — Anti-pest rinse before adding corals to display.
  • Compatibility — Likelihood species co-exist peacefully.

Maintenance & Upgrades

  • Trace Elements — Minor minerals replenished by water changes.
  • Detritus — Organic debris that breaks down to nutrients if not removed.
  • Vinegar Bath — 1:1 vinegar/water soak to remove mineral buildup.
  • Dosing Pump — Automates Alk/Ca/Mg additions.
  • Calibration — Re-checking instruments (probes, refractometer, heaters).
