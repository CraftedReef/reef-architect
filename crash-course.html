Before You Start: What is an Aquarium System?

A reef system is a box of water with controlled flow and filtration, heated to a stable temperature, and lit so corals can photosynthesize. Each tab starts with why it matters, then shows how to choose.

Plan & Gear

Why this matters: planning locks in volume, budget, and format (AIO vs sump) so every later choice fits and cables/plumbing have a clean path.

Start by defining your destination. A 20‚Äì40 gallon AIO makes a forgiving first reef: enough water volume to buffer mistakes, compact enough to manage. Decide early whether you‚Äôll run an AIO or a sump; both work, but a sump adds room for a skimmer, refugium, and cleaner cable management. Sketch a budget in tiers‚Äîtank/stand, heater, return, flow, light, ATO, and basic test kits. Choose rock for structure first and looks second: stable, interlocked arches beat teetering ‚Äúcool‚Äù stacks. Think about where cords and hoses will live before the tank is wet. Reefing gets easier when the system itself does most of the work‚Äîand that starts with a deliberate plan.

Recap

  • Pick size/format (AIO vs sump) and set a realistic budget.
  • Core gear: return, heater (3‚Äì5 W/gal), flow, light, ATO, test kits.
  • Prioritize stable aquascape and clean cable/plumbing paths.

Water & Salt

Why this matters: pure water and consistent salt mix prevent early algae problems and keep chemistry predictable for corals.

Great reefs start with great water. Use RODI to bring total dissolved solids near zero; this prevents nuisance algae and unpredictability. Mix your chosen salt to 35 ppt (1.025‚Äì1.026 SG) and stick with it‚Äîconsistency is kinder to corals than constant brand-hopping. Match your foundation parameters to your long-term plan: if you aim for LPS and softies, you can live with moderate alkalinity and minimal dosing; if SPS is in your future, choose a salt and routine that supports stable alkalinity from day one. Keep a refractometer calibrated‚Äîsalinity drift is the silent tank wrecker.

Recap

  • RODI water (near 0 TDS) + consistent salt at 35 ppt (1.025‚Äì1.026 SG).
  • Pick a salt that fits your dosing/stability goals.
  • Calibrate refractometer; prevent salinity creep.

Setup Day

Why this matters: smart layout and leak-check on day one saves headaches later and protects livestock when you add it.

Dry-fit everything before a drop of water touches the glass. Level the stand, set the tank, and confirm the aquascape is rock-solid‚Äîpush on it from different angles. Rinse sand until the water runs mostly clear. Fill with mixed saltwater, start the heater and return, and let the system run while you check for leaks. Keep lights off; you‚Äôre growing bacteria, not algae. If you have an ATO, set it up now so evaporation never becomes a guessing game. This is the calm before the biological storm‚Äîuse it to make tidy, safe cable paths with drip loops.

Recap

  • Level stand; dry-fit plumbing; lock aquascape; rinse sand.
  • Fill with 35 ppt saltwater; start heat/return; leak check.
  • Lights off; set up ATO and clean cable management.

Cycle & First Tests

Why this matters: the nitrogen cycle makes the tank safe for life. Rushing this step kills fish and stalls progress.

Seed the tank with bottled bacteria and an ammonia source, then watch the nitrogen story unfold. Ammonia rises first, nitrite follows, and nitrate appears last. Test every few days and be patient. The tank is ready when ammonia and nitrite read zero and nitrate is present. Do a partial water change to bring nitrate into a comfortable range. Early diatoms and algae are normal‚Äîresist the urge to overreact. Stability is built on measured inputs and time, not on constant tinkering.

Flow, Filtration & Nutrients

Why this matters: flow keeps detritus suspended for removal and delivers oxygen to corals. Target total in-tank flow of 10‚Äì20√ó (soft/LPS) or 30‚Äì60√ó (SPS).

Corals breathe with your flow. Aim for broad, randomized movement that sweeps detritus off the rock and keeps water exchanging across coral surfaces without blasting tissue. Mechanical filtration (floss or socks) polishes the water; a skimmer adds gas exchange and nutrient export as bioload grows. Many tanks thrive with simple export‚Äîregular water changes and prudent feeding‚Äîwhile others add refugiums or reactors later. Pick nutrient targets you can actually maintain and stick to them; consistent nitrate and phosphate levels make coral coloration and growth far more predictable.

Recap

  • Randomized, non-laminar flow; no dead spots.
  • Mechanical filter ‚Üí optional skimmer ‚Üí optional refugium/reactors.
  • Set realistic NO‚ÇÉ/PO‚ÇÑ targets and maintain them consistently.

Lighting & PAR On-Ramp

Why this matters: corals photosynthesize. Proper PAR and a slow ramp prevent bleaching while still fueling growth.

Light is food for corals‚Äîand too much, too fast, burns them. Start low and ramp up slowly. Target ranges (as general guides): softies under ~100‚Äì150 PAR, many LPS around ~75‚Äì200 PAR, and SPS commonly ~200‚Äì350+ PAR depending on species and placement. Keep spectrum stable; resist the urge to chase ‚Äúlooks‚Äù daily. A consistent photoperiod and gradual intensity changes do more for coral health than endless schedule tweaks. If you can borrow a PAR meter, do it‚Äîit replaces guesswork with confidence.

Recap

  • Ramp intensity slowly; maintain a consistent photoperiod.
  • Softies ~<100‚Äì150 PAR; LPS ~75‚Äì200; SPS ~200‚Äì350+ (rule-of-thumb).
  • Keep spectrum stable; measure PAR if possible.

Stocking & Acclimation

Why this matters: stocking order and quarantine reduce aggression and disease, protecting your investment.

Add life in a calm, deliberate sequence. Start with a modest clean-up crew to graze film algae and stir the sand. Introduce hardy, compatible fish that fit your tank size and aquascape‚Äîterritory matters. Quarantine when you can; prevention beats treatment in a display. When the tank is stable, place beginner corals low and let them tell you how they feel before you raise light or flow. Feed with intention: enough to nourish, not enough to pollute. The best stocking plan is the one you can sustain week after week.

Recap

  • Order: CUC ‚Üí hardy fish ‚Üí beginner corals ‚Üí expand slowly.
  • Mind compatibility, territory, and total capacity.
  • Quarantine when possible; feed deliberately.

Maintenance & Upgrades

Why this matters: small, regular habits keep nutrients stable and equipment reliable so corals thrive long-term.

Reefing thrives on rhythm. Each week, test nitrate and phosphate, clean the glass, and blow detritus from the rock. Do water changes when nutrients drift or trace elements need refreshing. Each month, service pumps and skimmer, replace or recharge media, and sanity-check heater accuracy. Add gear when it solves a real, repeating problem‚Äîlike a refugium to smooth nutrients or a dosing pump to hold alkalinity steady. Upgrades should make the tank more stable, not more complicated. Change one thing at a time and let the system respond before changing anything else.

Recap

  • Weekly: NO‚ÇÉ/PO‚ÇÑ test, glass clean, detritus blow-off, water change as needed.
  • Monthly+: clean pumps/skimmer; refresh carbon/media; verify heater/probes.
  • Upgrade only to increase stability; one change at a time.
