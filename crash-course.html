Crash Course into Reefing

This crash course is organized into eight phases to introduce the basics of planning, building, and caring for your new reef aquarium as it matures. Before You Start: What is an Aquarium System?

A reef aquarium is a miniature ocean, a living ecosystem that we personally create within our homes. The water acts as the ocean itself, carrying oxygen and nutrients to everything that lives within it. FIlters and pums keep the water moving and clean, while the heater keeps it warm like the tropics and the lights provide the energy corals need to grow just as sunlight fuels reefs in nature Every part of the system that you choose will work together to keep life stable. In this guide, every section will explain why these pieces matter and how to choose them for your reef.

Before water, fish, or coral ever touch our aquarium, you‚Äôre designing an environment that has to stay balanced every day. 1. Understanding the Foundation

Think of your aquarium as a living machine. Every piece of equipment plays a role in keeping the environment balanced: the tank holds your ecosystem; filtration removes waste; flow delivers oxygen and nutrients; heating maintains temperatures; and lighting fuels coral growth just as sunlight feeds reefs in nature. When these systems work together, you create stability and stability is the key to success.

2. Choosing Your Tank Type

Start small enough to manage, but large enough to be forgiving. For most beginners, a 20‚Äì40 gallon All-In-One (AIO) tank is ideal. It‚Äôs compact, tidy, and includes built-in chambers for your heater, pump, and filter media ‚Äî no plumbing required.

If you have more room and want flexibility, consider a sump system. A sump is a second tank hidden inside the stand that holds extra water volume and equipment. It keeps the display clean, increases stability, and allows for add-ons like a skimmer or refugium. The trade-off is higher cost and a bit more setup work.

Water & Salt

Consistency is key! Every reef lives and dies by water quality. Tap water and inconsistent salt mixes create unpredictable chemistry that leads to algae and coral stress. Start with pure RODI water and keep a consistent salt recipe so your animals can trust the water, day after day.

1. Start with Pure Water

For reef aquariums, tap water is a trap. It can contain chlorine/chloramine, nitrates, phosphates, and metals that feed algae and harm invertebrates. Use RODI water (Reverse Osmosis + Deionization) to bring TDS (Total Dissolved Solids) near zero before any salt is added.

Term Meaning Why It Matters
RODI Filtration that removes nearly all dissolved solids from tap water. Prevents nuisance algae and chemistry swings from contaminants.
TDS Total Dissolved Solids (ppm) remaining after filtration. Keep ‚â§ 5 ppm; ideally 0 ppm for reef tanks.
DI Resin Final RODI stage that ‚Äúpolishes‚Äù water by removing leftover ions. Replace when your TDS starts to rise to maintain purity.

Getting RODI water: buy pre-filtered RODI from a local fish store, or install a small 4-stage home RODI unit (faucet or hose hookup). It‚Äôs one of the best long-term investments in the hobby.

2. Mix Your Saltwater

The ocean averages 35 ppt salinity (1.025‚Äì1.026 SG). Mix saltwater in a separate bucket/barrel, never in the display! Use a small pump for circulation and a heater to ~78 ¬∞F (25.5 ¬∞C) while mixing.

  1. Fill a clean container with RODI water.
  2. Add a small circulation pump and heater (to ~78 ¬∞F).
  3. Slowly add your salt mix while water circulates.
  4. Test salinity with a refractometer (more accurate than swing hydrometers).
  5. Follow mixing instructions for your choice of salt to fully dissolve and stabilize pH.
  6. Adjust salinity with RODI (to lower) or more salt (to raise).

3. Match Salt Mix to Your Plan

Different salts have different baseline alkalinity. Pick one that fits your long-term livestock goals and stick with it. Remember staying consistent is extremely important. Switching salts often will throw your tank off-balance and will only cause your fish and corals to stress.

5. Maintenance Tips

  • Use RODI only for top-off (evaporation). Never add saltwater for top-off.
  • Shoot for consisten 15-20% water changes every 2 weeks to replenish nutrients absorbed by your corals
  • Store mixed saltwater with a lid and small circulation pump if not used immediately.
  • Keep spare DI resin, RODI filters, and 35 ppt calibration fluid on hand.
  • Change salt brands only if you have a reason ‚Äî consistency helps corals thrive.

Cycle & First Tests

The nitrogen cycle is the foundation of reef life. Before fish or coral can survive, your system needs beneficial bacteria that convert toxic waste into safer forms. Rushing this process ‚Äî or skipping it ‚Äî leads to deadly ammonia spikes that can wipe out everything you‚Äôve built. Cycling is how you teach your tank to breathe. This is where patience pays off: biology, not equipment, decides when your tank is ready.

Flow, Filtration & Nutrients

Why this matters: flow keeps detritus suspended for removal and delivers oxygen to corals. Target total in-tank flow of 10‚Äì20√ó (soft/LPS) or 30‚Äì60√ó (SPS).

Corals breathe with your flow. Aim for broad, randomized movement that sweeps detritus off the rock and keeps water exchanging across coral surfaces without blasting tissue. Mechanical filtration (floss or socks) polishes the water; a skimmer adds gas exchange and nutrient export as bioload grows. Many tanks thrive with simple export‚Äîregular water changes and prudent feeding‚Äîwhile others add refugiums or reactors later. Pick nutrient targets you can actually maintain and stick to them; consistent nitrate and phosphate levels make coral coloration and growth far more predictable.

Recap

  • Randomized, non-laminar flow; no dead spots.
  • Mechanical filter ‚Üí optional skimmer ‚Üí optional refugium/reactors.
  • Set realistic NO‚ÇÉ/PO‚ÇÑ targets and maintain them consistently.

Lighting & PAR On-Ramp

Why this matters: corals photosynthesize. Proper PAR and a slow ramp prevent bleaching while still fueling growth.

Light is food for corals‚Äîand too much, too fast, burns them. Start low and ramp up slowly. Target ranges (as general guides): softies under ~100‚Äì150 PAR, many LPS around ~75‚Äì200 PAR, and SPS commonly ~200‚Äì350+ PAR depending on species and placement. Keep spectrum stable; resist the urge to chase ‚Äúlooks‚Äù daily. A consistent photoperiod and gradual intensity changes do more for coral health than endless schedule tweaks. If you can borrow a PAR meter, do it‚Äîit replaces guesswork with confidence.

Recap

  • Ramp intensity slowly; maintain a consistent photoperiod.
  • Softies ~<100‚Äì150 PAR; LPS ~75‚Äì200; SPS ~200‚Äì350+ (rule-of-thumb).
  • Keep spectrum stable; measure PAR if possible.

Stocking & Acclimation

Why this matters: stocking order and quarantine reduce aggression and disease, protecting your investment.

Add life in a calm, deliberate sequence. Start with a modest clean-up crew to graze film algae and stir the sand. Introduce hardy, compatible fish that fit your tank size and aquascape‚Äîterritory matters. Quarantine when you can; prevention beats treatment in a display. When the tank is stable, place beginner corals low and let them tell you how they feel before you raise light or flow. Feed with intention: enough to nourish, not enough to pollute. The best stocking plan is the one you can sustain week after week.

Recap

  • Order: CUC ‚Üí hardy fish ‚Üí beginner corals ‚Üí expand slowly.
  • Mind compatibility, territory, and total capacity.
  • Quarantine when possible; feed deliberately.

Maintenance & Upgrades

Why this matters: small, regular habits keep nutrients stable and equipment reliable so corals thrive long-term.

Reefing thrives on rhythm. Each week, test nitrate and phosphate, clean the glass, and blow detritus from the rock. Do water changes when nutrients drift or trace elements need refreshing. Each month, service pumps and skimmer, replace or recharge media, and sanity-check heater accuracy. Add gear when it solves a real, repeating problem‚Äîlike a refugium to smooth nutrients or a dosing pump to hold alkalinity steady. Upgrades should make the tank more stable, not more complicated. Change one thing at a time and let the system respond before changing anything else.

Recap

  • Weekly: NO‚ÇÉ/PO‚ÇÑ test, glass clean, detritus blow-off, water change as needed.
  • Monthly+: clean pumps/skimmer; refresh carbon/media; verify heater/probes.
  • Upgrade only to increase stability; one change at a time.
